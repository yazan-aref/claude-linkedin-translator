# LinkedIn Translator Claude Skill

**By Yazan Aref**

A specialized Claude skill I built for creating authentic, human-like LinkedIn content that passes anti-detection rules and maximizes engagement through strategic formatting, humanization, and algorithmic optimization.

## Overview

I created the **linkedin-translator** skill to help me—and now you—generate LinkedIn posts that:
- Sound genuinely human (not AI-generated)
- Vary in length and style to avoid algorithmic detection
- Strategically leverage LinkedIn's algorithm through timed comments and link placement
- Maintain consistent engagement patterns across different content types

## The Problem I Was Solving

LinkedIn's algorithm heavily favors posts that generate immediate engagement and conversation. But I noticed something: most AI-generated content gets flagged because it follows patterns that are *too* consistent, *too* polished, *too* uniform. 

So I built this skill to teach Claude how to write posts that follow proven posting patterns while maintaining authentic human voice—the only real way to avoid detection and actually boost visibility. The secret wasn't hiding the AI; it was embedding real human patterns into what the AI creates.

## Post Format Template

This is the structure I discovered works best:

```
[HOOK — 1 sentence that stops the scroll]

[BODY — 3-5 blocks of 1-3 sentences]
[Each block = 1 idea]
[White space between blocks]
[At least 1 parenthetical or "thinking out loud"]

[CLOSING — Signature, question, mic-drop, or imperative]

#hashtag1 #hashtag2 #hashtag3
```

### Key Structural Rules

- **Hook**: A single, compelling sentence designed to capture attention immediately
- **Body blocks**: I break ideas into separate blocks separated by white space for readability
- **Parentheticals**: I always include at least one aside or thinking-out-loud moment—it's what makes writing feel human
- **Closing**: End with a signature, question to prompt engagement, bold statement, or call-to-action
- **Hashtags**: 3 relevant hashtags at the end

## Length Variation (Critical for Detection Avoidance)

**This is the #1 anti-detection principle I discovered.** The moment I realized that mixing post lengths across my week was essential, everything changed. Uniform post length is a dead giveaway—it's the most obvious AI detection signal.

### Weekly Mix (7 posts)

Here's what I aim for:

- **1 micro-post**: 200–400 characters
- **4–5 standard posts**: 600–1000 characters each
- **1 long-form post**: 1000–1300 characters

I never post the same length twice in a row, and I mix short and long throughout the week. The randomness is what feels human.

## Humanization Rules

These are the 5 rules I developed to keep a profile authentic and undetectable:

### 1. One Parenthetical Per Post

I always include a parenthetical thought that shows inner processing:
- "(honestly, I didn't expect this)"
- "(and here's where it gets interesting)"
- "(still figuring this out, but…)"

Parentheticals are how humans think out loud. They're invisible to automation.

### 2. Informal Markers (2–3 per post)

I use casual, natural speech patterns throughout:
- **Sentence fragments**: "Not as easy as it sounds." instead of complete sentences
- **Self-questions**: "Does it work? It works." or "Why? Because…"
- **Casual transitions**: "So here's the thing:" or "And that's when it clicked."

### 3. Imperfection

I avoid robot-like perfection. Real humans show:
- Interrupted thoughts or course corrections
- Doubts or questions mid-point
- Grammatical variation (not all polished)

This is where I let my actual thinking process show.

### 4. Vulnerability (Every 2 Weeks)

I post something that went wrong or caused frustration:
- A mistake I made and learned from
- A project that didn't go as planned
- A doubt or struggle I'm working through

Vulnerability builds relatability and trust faster than anything else.

### 5. Length Variation (Ongoing)

I mix 200–1300 characters across my posting schedule. Never uniform lengths—that's the biggest giveaway of automation.

## Strategic Comment Protocol

This was a game-changer for me: LinkedIn's algorithm amplifies posts where the author comments within 30 minutes of posting. **I learned that every post needs a first auto-comment** following strategic rules:

### Comment Strategy by Post Type

I discovered that different post types benefit from different comment approaches:

| Post Type | Day | Comment Content | Link Placement |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Value post** | Tue, Wed, Fri, Sat | Follow-up question or complementary insight | No link (value only) |
| **Case study** | Thu | Complementary data + soft consultation CTA | Include soft link |
| **Call-to-action (CTA)** | Sun | Direct offer or announcement | Direct product/service link |
| **Behind the scenes** | Mon | Story or process insight | Blog post link (if relevant) |

### Critical Rule: Link Placement

**Links go in the first comment, NEVER in the caption.** 

This was the breakthrough for me. LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes posts with external links in the main caption. When I started placing links in my first comment instead, everything changed:
- I avoided algorithmic suppression
- Traffic still flowed (engaged readers click)
- The feed stayed conversation-focused, which the algorithm loves

### Timing

- I post at optimal time for my audience
- I comment within **15–30 minutes** to trigger the algorithm boost
- That first comment generates the most engagement and positions the link naturally

## Weekly Content Calendar Structure

This is the rhythm I follow for a balanced week:

- **Monday**: Behind the scenes (process, journey, workspace insight)
- **Tuesday**: Value post (tip, insight, lesson learned)
- **Wednesday**: Value post (complementary idea or data)
- **Thursday**: Case study (specific example with results)
- **Friday**: Value post (inspiration, reflection, forward-thinking)
- **Saturday**: Value post (weekend perspective or longer reflection)
- **Sunday**: CTA post (direct offer, announcement, or invitation to action)

I've found this structure keeps my feed diverse without feeling forced.

## Usage Examples

### Micro-Post (200–400 chars)

```
The best founders I know stopped asking "Is this feasible?" and started asking "Is this worth it?"

(That shift changes everything.)

#entrepreneurship #strategy #startups
```

First comment (within 30 min):
> "What's been your hardest shift in asking the right questions? Would love to hear what changed for you."

### Standard Post (600–1000 chars)

```
I spent 3 years optimizing for growth metrics and completely missed what actually mattered.

More users, more revenue, more engagement—but the conversations felt hollow. Something was off.

(And honestly, I couldn't put my finger on it at first.)

Then a customer said something that stopped me cold: "I don't feel like a person to you, I feel like a number."

That one line rewired how I approach building.

Now? I optimize for meaning first. Growth follows.

#productmanagement #leadership #humancentered
```

First comment (within 30 min):
> "What's one time you realized your metrics were pointing you in the wrong direction?"

### Long-Form Post (1000–1300 chars)

```
The best feedback I ever got came from someone who wasn't even in my industry.

A mentor told me: "Stop explaining what you do and start showing what it means."

I was in tech, talking to investors about metrics and scaling. Technical, clean, focused. All the right words.

But not one person actually understood why I cared.

So I rewrote everything. Instead of "AI-driven market segmentation," I wrote: "Helping small businesses know their customers like real humans do." Instead of "retention curves," I showed stories of what that meant for a florist and a personal trainer.

The conversations changed immediately. Not more conversations—better ones. People who actually got it.

(The irony? I became 10x more credible by being 10x more human.)

The best positioning isn't about being the smartest person in the room. It's about being the clearest.

What's one thing you're overcomplicating right now?

#storytelling #positioning #communication
```

First comment (within 30 min):
> "Share a time when you simplified something and it actually worked better—or backfired spectacularly."

## Implementation Tips

Here's how I actually use this system:

1. **Plan a week at a time**: I create all 7 posts upfront, mix lengths deliberately, and assign post types to days
2. **Draft all first comments**: I write them simultaneously and schedule for auto-posting 15–30 min after each post
3. **Vary sentence structure**: I use fragments, questions, and conversational asides—not just full sentences
4. **Sprinkle in vulnerability**: Every 2 weeks, I share a struggle or mistake (it's what builds real engagement)
5. **Test and track**: I monitor which post types, lengths, and comment prompts get best engagement and iterate
6. **Maintain consistency**: I post regularly (same days/times) to train both my audience and the algorithm

## Why This Works

I learned these principles through building LinkedIn presence myself:

- **Human voice**: Parentheticals, imperfection, and vulnerability are signatures of real thinking—not automation
- **Algorithm-friendly**: Length variation avoids detection flags; first comments trigger the amplification I need
- **Engagement-focused**: Strategic questions and complementary information in comments drive meaningful interaction, not vanity metrics
- **Sustainable**: I built this on patterns real humans actually use, not tricks that'll get flagged in 6 months
